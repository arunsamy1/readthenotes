No, there is no ETF that concentrates on Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC) companies alone.

The market for SOFC technology is currently too niche and specialized to support its own dedicated fund. Instead, if you want exposure to SOFC companies through an exchange-traded fund, you have to look at Hydrogen and Fuel Cell ETFs or broader Clean Energy ETFs.

Even within those funds, you will only find a couple of pure-play SOFC companies because very few public firms focus exclusively on that specific technology.

The closest options to give you exposure include:

1. Dedicated Hydrogen & Fuel Cell ETFs

These funds target the entire hydrogen value chain, meaning they bundle SOFC companies alongside companies making Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) fuel cells, electrolyzers, and industrial hydrogen gas.

  • Global X Hydrogen ETF (HYDR): This is the primary pure-play option left for US investors. It tracks the Solactive Global Hydrogen Index.
  • VanEck Hydrogen Economy UCITS ETF (HDRO) / L&G Hydrogen Economy ETF: These are prominent options if you have access to European (UCITS) markets.

How SOFC Fits Into These ETFs

Because there isn't a standalone SOFC ETF, the best way to play the space through a fund is to look at the ETF's heaviest allocations. The macro-trend for data center power has recently pushed specific SOFC players to the top of these funds:

  • Bloom Energy ($BE$): They are the undisputed global leader in Solid Oxide Fuel Cell commercialization (known for their "Bloom Energy Servers"). In most hydrogen ETFs like HYDR, Bloom Energy is typically the largest single holding (often making up 14% to 16% of the entire fund).
  • Ceres Power ($CERS$ / $CPWHF$): A UK-based pioneer in fuel cell technology that licenses out its proprietary steel-cell SOFC design to global manufacturing partners. It is a frequent holding in global hydrogen portfolios.

2. Broader Clean Energy ETFs

If you don't want a 100% hydrogen-focused fund, certain clean energy ETFs carry high concentrations of SOFC leaders due to their sheer market cap and revenue.

  • First Trust NASDAQ Clean Edge Green Energy Index Fund (QCLN): While this fund includes electric vehicles, solar, and wind, it historically heavily weights Bloom Energy as one of its top allocations because of its massive grid-support and data center contracts.
Summary: If you want maximum exposure to Solid Oxide Fuel Cells via an ETF, the Global X Hydrogen ETF (HYDR) is your closest bet, primarily because it places such a massive, double-digit weighting on Bloom Energy. If you want a 100% pure focus on SOFC technology, your only real option is to buy the individual stocks directly.
